## Single-line hook

> "这一次他没有再站在岸上看。这一次他跟着沉。"
>
> *"This time he was no longer standing on the shore watching. This time he sank with him."*

## Creative intent — the three-strike pattern

This passage was chosen as the show piece because of its triple-strike construction:

1. **Emotional strike** — the two ends of the rope: forcing the other man's hand open vs. letting go of the gunwale himself
2. **Craft strike (hidden nail)** — the flash of past-lives imagery (black water / snow light / the door you cannot see), *delivered without explanation*
3. **Visual strike at chapter end** — under the water surface, one rope, two people sinking together

The character relationship is **legible from action alone** — no Ch01 context required. This is the design property that makes the excerpt portable for an unfamiliar reader, which is why it was selected over more atmospheric or expository passages.

## What this case shows about the workflow

- **The outline team** debated the structural choice ("let him sink with him" vs. "save him then break with him") at the volume level. Choosing the former required cross-arbitration between the divergent staging proposals and the aesthete's rhythm reading. See `examples/workflow-template/4-agent-outline/` for the orchestration pattern.
- **The prose review team** caught and removed three "author-intrusion" lines from earlier drafts, preserving the structure of the three strikes intact. See `examples/workflow-template/5-agent-review/` (v0.2 land).
- The single-line hook survived the **voice-check** filter across multiple draft passes — it was the only line that passed the "remove the explanation, keep the action" rule.

## What this case is not

Not a chapter to be appreciated in isolation as fiction. The excerpt is **a portfolio specimen**: it demonstrates that the multi-agent workflow can produce a passage where character, plot causality, and aesthetic compression all hold together in one beat.
